# Fruits of the Spirit: A 9-Week Youth Ministry Course
**Target Audience:** Secondary School Students (Aged 11–16)  
**Context:** UK Youth Groups, School Christian Unions, or Church Youth Clubs  
**Duration:** 9 Weeks (60 Minutes per Session)  
**Course Aim:** To help students understand and experience the character of the Holy Spirit in their daily lives.

---

## Course Overview
This course explores Galatians 5:22-23, breaking down the nine attributes of the Holy Spirit into practical, relatable lessons for UK teenagers. Each session is designed to be interactive, biblically grounded, and culturally relevant, moving from theological understanding to real-world application in school, home, and social settings.

## Lesson 1: Love (Agape)
**Theme:** The Foundation of Everything

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will understand that biblical love is a choice to seek the good of others, not just a feeling, and will identify one way to show sacrificial love this week.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Love Language Quiz**
    *   Hand out a simple quiz matching actions to 'Love Languages' (Words, Time, Gifts, Help, Touch).
    *   Ask students to share which one they prefer receiving.
    *   **Debrief:** Explain that while we like receiving love differently, biblical love is about *giving* what the other person needs, not just what feels easy for us.
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "A new command I give you: Love one another. As I have loved you, so you must love one another." (John 13:34)
    *   **Context:** In Greek, there are many words for love. The Bible uses *agape* here. It isn't about romance (*eros*) or just liking someone (*philia*). It is a decision to act for someone's best interest.
    *   **Illustration:** Think of a rugby player who passes the ball to a teammate in a better position to score, rather than trying to score themselves. That is *agape*—putting someone else's success above your own glory.
    *   **The Cross:** The ultimate example is Jesus. He loved us when we were unlovely. "But God demonstrates his own love for us in this: While we were still sinners, Christ died for us." (Romans 5:8)
    *   **Point:** Love is the soil from which all other fruits grow. You cannot have true patience or kindness without love.
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'The Secret Servant'. Challenge students to do one act of sacrificial love this week anonymously (e.g., clean the kitchen without being asked, send an encouraging text to someone who is lonely).
    *   **Devotion:** Lead a short prayer asking God to show them one person who is hard to love, and to give them the strength to show kindness to that person anyway.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   What is the difference between 'liking' someone and 'loving' them biblically?
    *   Why is it easier to love people who are nice to us?
    *   Can you think of a time when someone showed you love when you didn't deserve it?
    *   How does knowing God loves you help you love others?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Be sensitive to students who may feel unloved at home. Ensure language is inclusive.
    *   **Preparation:** Print quizzes beforehand.
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** If a student shares about family breakdown, listen actively and follow safeguarding protocols for follow-up support.

## Lesson 2: Joy
**Theme:** Happiness vs. Deep-Down Gladness

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will distinguish between circumstantial happiness and supernatural joy, and learn to find joy even in difficult times.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Smile Challenge**
    *   Pair students up. One tries to make the other laugh without touching them for 60 seconds.
    *   **Debrief:** Discuss how easy it is to change mood based on external fun. Ask: "Can you feel joy when nothing funny is happening?"
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "Consider it pure joy, my brothers and sisters, whenever you face trials of many kinds." (James 1:2)
    *   **Context:** Happiness depends on 'happenings'. Joy depends on God. It is a fruit of the Spirit, meaning it grows inside you regardless of the weather outside.
    *   **Illustration:** Imagine your phone battery. Happiness is like using apps—it drains quickly. Joy is like being plugged into the charger (God); it keeps going even when you're doing hard tasks.
    *   **Example:** Paul and Silas sang hymns in prison. They were in pain and unjustly treated, yet they had joy because they knew God was with them.
    *   **Point:** Joy is not ignoring pain; it is knowing God is sovereign over the pain.
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'Joy Journal'. Hand out cards. Students write down three things they are thankful for right now, even if they are stressed about exams or friends.
    *   **Devotion:** Read Nehemiah 8:10 ("The joy of the Lord is your strength"). Pray for strength for the week ahead.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   What is the biggest difference between being happy and being joyful?
    *   Why do you think God asks us to have joy even when things are tough?
    *   How does your mood affect your friends at school?
    *   What steals your joy the most (social media, grades, arguments)?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Be aware that some students may be dealing with depression. Clarify that biblical joy is not a command to ignore mental health struggles.
    *   **Preparation:** Prepare cards/pens for the journal activity.
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** If a student expresses hopelessness, ensure you have contact details for school counsellors or Christian support lines ready.

## Lesson 3: Peace
**Theme:** Calm in the Chaos

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will learn that biblical peace is not just the absence of conflict but the presence of God, and will practise one technique to manage anxiety.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Storm in a Cup**
    *   Give each student a clear cup of water and a spoon. Have them stir it vigorously to make a 'storm'. Then stop and watch it settle.
    *   **Debrief:** Discuss what helps the water settle (stopping the stirring). Peace often requires us to stop 'stirring' our worries.
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "Peace I leave with you; my peace I give you. I do not give to you as the world gives." (John 14:27)
    *   **Context:** The Hebrew word *Shalom* means wholeness, completeness, and health. It's not just 'no fighting'; it's everything working as it should.
    *   **Illustration:** Think of noise-cancelling headphones. The world is loud (exams, drama, news), but God's peace blocks out the noise so you can hear His voice.
    *   **Example:** Jesus sleeping on the boat during a storm. He had peace because He knew who He was and who was in control.
    *   **Point:** We have peace *with* God (because of Jesus) and the peace *of* God (in our minds).
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'The Worry Box'. Students write a current worry on a piece of paper, fold it, and put it in a box at the front. Symbolise giving it to God.
    *   **Devotion:** Lead a breathing prayer. Inhale: "Prince of Peace". Exhale: "My Anxiety".
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   What situations cause you the most stress or anxiety?
    *   How do you usually react when things go wrong (anger, shutting down, panic)?
    *   What does it mean to be a 'peacemaker' at school?
    *   How can prayer help bring peace to your mind?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Anxiety is common in teens. Do not trivialise worries.
    *   **Preparation:** Bring a box and paper scraps.
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** If a student shares about serious conflict at home, note it for follow-up. Do not force them to share publicly.

## Lesson 4: Patience
**Theme:** Waiting Well

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will understand patience as active endurance rather than passive waiting, and will identify an area where they need to trust God's timing.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The One-Minute Silence**
    *   Ask everyone to close their eyes and sit in silence for exactly one minute. No phones, no talking.
    *   **Debrief:** Ask how it felt. Was it boring? Frustrating? Explain that patience is often uncomfortable.
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "Be patient, then, brothers and sisters, until the Lord's coming." (James 5:7)
    *   **Context:** The Greek word *makrothumia* means 'long-tempered'. It is the opposite of having a short fuse.
    *   **Illustration:** Think of loading a video game on slow Wi-Fi. You can mash the buttons (impatience) or wait for it to load (patience). Mashings breaks nothing; waiting allows the game to work properly.
    *   **Example:** Abraham waited decades for a son. Joseph waited years in prison before becoming a leader. God's timing is rarely our timing.
    *   **Point:** Patience protects relationships. It stops us from exploding when friends annoy us.
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'The Pause Button'. Teach students to count to five before responding when they are angry. Practise this in role-play scenarios (e.g., someone bumps into you).
    *   **Devotion:** Read Galatians 6:9 ("Let us not become weary in doing good"). Pray for strength to keep going when things are slow.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   What is the hardest thing for you to wait for (results, replies, growing up)?
    *   How does impatience hurt your friendships?
    *   Why do you think God makes us wait sometimes?
    *   Who is the most patient person you know?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Ensure role-plays remain respectful and do not trigger past conflicts.
    *   **Preparation:** Prepare scenario cards for role-play.
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** Be mindful of students with ADHD or impulse control issues; frame patience as a skill to learn, not a moral failure.

## Lesson 5: Kindness
**Theme:** More Than Being Nice

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will recognise kindness as an active choice to help others, even when it costs them something, and will plan one act of intentional kindness.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Compliment Chain**
    *   Sit in a circle. One person compliments the person to their right, who then compliments the next, and so on.
    *   **Debrief:** Discuss how it feels to give and receive kindness. Is it easier to give or receive?
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "Be kind and compassionate to one another, forgiving each other, just as in Christ God forgave you." (Ephesians 4:32)
    *   **Context:** Biblical kindness (*chrestotes*) is useful goodness. It's not just smiling; it's helping.
    *   **Illustration:** Being 'nice' is holding the door open. Being 'kind' is helping someone carry their heavy trays in the canteen when they are struggling.
    *   **Example:** The Good Samaritan. He crossed cultural boundaries and spent his own money to help a stranger.
    *   **Point:** Kindness breaks cycles of negativity. It is a weapon against bullying and exclusion.
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'Kindness Coupons'. Students create vouchers they can give to others (e.g., 'I will listen to you for 10 mins', 'I will help you with maths').
    *   **Devotion:** Read Ruth 2:12. Pray for eyes to see people who are overlooked at school.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   What is the difference between being nice and being kind?
    *   Why is it hard to be kind to someone who has been mean to you?
    *   How can we be kind to people online, not just in person?
    *   Who is someone in your life who needs kindness right now?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Monitor group dynamics to ensure no one is left out during the activity.
    *   **Preparation:** Provide paper and coloured pens for coupons.
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** Discuss cyberbullying carefully. Encourage reporting serious issues to adults.

## Lesson 6: Goodness
**Theme:** Integrity in Action

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will define goodness as moral integrity and will commit to doing the right thing even when no one is watching.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Honesty Test**
    *   Present hypothetical scenarios (e.g., "You find a £10 note on the floor. No one saw. What do you do?"). Vote by moving to different sides of the room.
    *   **Debrief:** Discuss why doing the right thing can be difficult when there is no reward.
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "For the fruit of the light consists in all goodness, righteousness and truth." (Ephesians 5:9)
    *   **Context:** Goodness is about moral excellence. It is linked to truth. You cannot be good if you are living a lie.
    *   **Illustration:** Think of a referee in a football match. They must make the right call even if the crowd boos them. Goodness is following the rules of God's Kingdom even when the world says otherwise.
    *   **Example:** Joseph refusing Potiphar's wife. He said, "How could I do such a wicked thing and sin against God?" He cared more about God's view than his boss's view.
    *   **Point:** Goodness silences criticism. When you live with integrity, people respect you even if they disagree with your faith.
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'The Integrity Check'. Students reflect on areas where they might be cutting corners (homework, honesty with parents, online behaviour). Write down one change to make.
    *   **Devotion:** Read Psalm 15. Pray for courage to stand alone if necessary.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   What does 'integrity' mean to you?
    *   When is the hardest time to do the right thing?
    *   How does cheating (in exams or games) affect your character?
    *   Why does God care about what we do in private?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Create a safe space where students can admit struggles without fear of immediate punishment.
    *   **Preparation:** Prepare scenario cards for the ice-breaker.
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** If a student admits to serious wrongdoing (theft, harm), follow safeguarding reporting procedures.

## Lesson 7: Faithfulness
**Theme:** Reliability in a Changing World

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will understand faithfulness as consistency and loyalty, and will identify one commitment they can keep this week.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Trust Fall (Modified)**
    *   In pairs, one person closes their eyes and walks slowly while the other guides them verbally through an obstacle course of chairs.
    *   **Debrief:** Discuss how it felt to rely on someone else. Faithfulness is about being someone others can rely on.
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "Great is your faithfulness; your mercies begin afresh each morning." (Lamentations 3:23)
    *   **Context:** Faithfulness (*pistis*) means loyalty and reliability. It is keeping your word.
    *   **Illustration:** Think of a bus timetable. If the bus never arrives when it says it will, you stop trusting it. God is always on time. We are called to be like that too.
    *   **Example:** Daniel praying three times a day even when it was illegal. He was consistent. Ruth staying with Naomi even when it was hard.
    *   **Point:** God values faithfulness over success. You might not be the best at football, but you can be the most faithful teammate.
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'The Commitment Contract'. Students write down one thing they will be faithful in this week (e.g., prayer, arriving on time, helping at home).
    *   **Devotion:** Read Matthew 25:21 ("Well done, good and faithful servant"). Pray for endurance.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   Why is it hard to keep commitments sometimes?
    *   How does it feel when a friend lets you down?
    *   What does it mean to be faithful to God during exam season?
    *   How does God's faithfulness to us help us be faithful to others?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Ensure students do not commit to unsafe things (e.g., meeting strangers).
    *   **Preparation:** Prepare paper for contracts.
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** Be aware of students with unstable home lives where 'consistency' is hard for them. Offer grace.

## Lesson 8: Gentleness
**Theme:** Strength Under Control

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will learn that gentleness is not weakness but controlled strength, and will practise responding calmly to irritation.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Egg Carry**
    *   Pairs must carry a raw egg (or balloon) on a spoon across the room without dropping it.
    *   **Debrief:** Discuss how much force was needed. Too much pressure breaks it; too little drops it. Gentleness is the right amount of power.
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart." (Matthew 11:29)
    *   **Context:** Gentleness (*prautes*) was used to describe a wild horse that had been trained. It still had power, but it was under control.
    *   **Illustration:** Think of a surgeon using a scalpel. The blade is sharp, but the hand is gentle. If the hand shakes, harm is done. Gentleness protects people.
    *   **Example:** Moses was described as the meekest man on earth, yet he led a nation and confronted Pharaoh. Jesus cleared the temple but healed the sick gently.
    *   **Point:** Gentleness disarms anger. When you respond softly to a shout, it stops the fight.
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'The Soft Response'. Role-play a conflict scenario where one person must respond gently instead of arguing back.
    *   **Devotion:** Read Galatians 6:1 ("Restore that person gently"). Pray for self-control in speech.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   Why do people often mistake gentleness for weakness?
    *   When is the hardest time to be gentle (when tired, hungry, stressed)?
    *   How can you be gentle when correcting a friend?
    *   How does Jesus' gentleness make you feel safe?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Ensure role-plays do not escalate into real arguments.
    *   **Preparation:** Bring eggs or balloons (have cleanup supplies ready).
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** Discuss domestic violence carefully if it arises; gentleness is not about staying in abusive situations.

## Lesson 9: Self-Control
**Theme:** Mastering Your Impulses

*   **Learning Objective:** Students will understand self-control as the power to say 'no' to good things at the wrong time, and will identify one habit they want to bring under God's control.
*   **Ice-Breaker (10 mins): The Marshmallow Test (Adapted)**
    *   Place a treat in front of each student. Tell them if they wait 5 minutes without eating it, they get two.
    *   **Debrief:** Discuss the struggle of impulse. What helped you wait? (Distraction, focusing on the reward).
*   **Bible Teaching (20 mins):**
    *   **Key Verse:** "For the Spirit God gave us does not make us timid, but gives us power, love and self-discipline." (2 Timothy 1:7)
    *   **Context:** Self-control (*enkrateia*) means having power over your own desires. It is not about suppressing emotions but managing them.
    *   **Illustration:** Think of a river. Water is good, but if it bursts its banks, it causes floods. Banks (self-control) allow the water to generate power instead of destruction.
    *   **Example:** Jesus fasting in the wilderness. He was hungry and had the power to turn stones to bread, but He controlled His appetite to obey God.
    *   **Point:** Self-control protects you from addiction (games, porn, substances) and helps you achieve goals (grades, sports).
*   **Application & Devotion (15 mins):**
    *   **Activity:** 'The Digital Detox Plan'. Students identify one screen habit they want to limit (e.g., no phone after 9 pm). Write a plan for how to achieve it.
    *   **Devotion:** Read 1 Corinthians 9:27 ("I discipline my body"). Pray for the Holy Spirit's power to say 'no'.
*   **Group Discussion (10 mins):**
    *   What is the hardest thing for you to control (temper, appetite, screen time, tongue)?
    *   Why do you think God wants us to have self-control?
    *   How does self-control help you in sports or exams?
    *   Where can you find power when you feel weak?
*   **Helper Guidance (5 mins):**
    *   **Safeguarding:** Be careful discussing addictions. Do not attempt to counsel serious addiction issues in a group setting; refer to professionals.
    *   **Preparation:** Bring treats for the ice-breaker (check for allergies).
    *   **Sensitive Topics:** If a student discloses substance abuse or self-harm, follow safeguarding protocols immediately.

## Course Conclusion & Next Steps
*   **Review:** Remind students that these are **Fruits**, not **Works**. They grow as we stay connected to Jesus (the Vine).
*   **Challenge:** Encourage students to pick one fruit to focus on for the next month.
*   **Prayer:** Close the course with a time of prayer, asking the Holy Spirit to continue growing these characteristics in each student.
*   **Resources:** Provide a handout with the key verses for each week so they can continue studying at home.

---

## General Helper Guidance for Leaders
*   **Safeguarding:** Always ensure two adults are present (two-deep leadership). Be aware of your diocese or organisation's safeguarding policy. If a child discloses harm, listen, do not promise confidentiality, and report to the Designated Safeguarding Lead immediately.
*   **Inclusivity:** Ensure activities are accessible for students with disabilities. Be mindful of students from non-Christian backgrounds; explain terms clearly.
*   **Environment:** Create a welcoming space. Have snacks available (UK teens love food!). Use music to set the tone.
*   **Follow-Up:** Collect contact details (with parental consent) to send weekly reminders or encouragement texts.
*   **Prayer:** Pray for each student by name before every session. The spiritual preparation is just as important as the lesson plan.
