### **1. Love (Galatians 5:22–23, 1 Corinthians 13:4–7, John 15:13, 1 John 4:7–8)**
**Activity: "The Love Languages Challenge"**
**Time**: 10–12 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Printed "Love Languages" cards (Words of Affirmation, Acts of Service, Receiving Gifts, Quality Time, Physical Touch)
- Pens and paper
- Timer

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that love isn’t just a feeling—it’s an action. Briefly introduce the **5 Love Languages** (Gary Chapman) as a way to show love to others.
2. **Game Setup**: Split students into small groups (3–4 people). Give each group a set of "Love Languages" cards.
3. **Challenge**: Each group has **2 minutes** to come up with **one practical way** to show love using each language (e.g., "Words of Affirmation: Write a note to a friend telling them why you appreciate them").
4. **Share**: Groups take turns sharing their ideas. Award points for creativity (e.g., "Most UK-relevant idea: ‘Make a cuppa for your mate who’s stressed about exams’").
5. **Reflection**: Ask, *"Which love language do you think is hardest to show in school? Why?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Provide sentence starters (e.g., "Acts of Service: I could help my friend by...").
- **Extended**: Have students **act out their ideas** in a quick skit.
- **Large Groups**: Split into pairs and have them discuss **one way they can show love this week**.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British examples (e.g., "Quality Time: Invite a classmate to watch the football match with you").
- Reference UK pop culture (e.g., "Receiving Gifts: Like when Harry Potter gave Ron a Chocolate Frog to cheer him up").

### **2. Joy (Galatians 5:22–23, Nehemiah 8:10, Romans 15:13, James 1:2–3)**
**Activity: "The Joy Playlist"**
**Time**: 10–12 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Whiteboard or large paper
- Sticky notes or index cards
- Pens
- Phone/Bluetooth speaker (optional)

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that joy isn’t about being happy all the time—it’s about **finding strength in God** (Nehemiah 8:10). Ask, *"What’s one song that makes you feel joyful, even when you’re stressed?"*
2. **Game Setup**: Write **"Joy Playlist"** on the board. Give each student a sticky note.
3. **Challenge**: Students write down **one song** that brings them joy (e.g., worship music, a hype song, a nostalgic tune). They can also include **one reason** why it brings them joy (e.g., "Reminds me of my nan," "Makes me want to dance").
4. **Share**: Students stick their notes on the board. Read a few aloud and **play a 10-second clip** of the most popular songs (if time allows).
5. **Reflection**: Ask, *"How can we choose joy even when life’s tough? What’s one thing you can do this week to cultivate joy?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Provide a list of songs to choose from (e.g., Hillsong Young & Free, Stormzy, Ed Sheeran).
- **Extended**: Have students **create a "Joy Playlist"** on Spotify and share it with the group.
- **Large Groups**: Split into smaller groups to discuss their songs before sharing with the whole group.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Reference UK artists (e.g., "‘Goodness of God’ by Bethel Music, ‘Blinded by Your Grace’ by Stormzy").
- Use British humor (e.g., "Even if your song is ‘Baby Shark,’ we’ll allow it—joy is joy!").

### **3. Peace (Galatians 5:22–23, Philippians 4:6–7, John 14:27, Isaiah 26:3)**
**Activity: "The Peace Puzzle"**
**Time**: 12–15 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Printed puzzle pieces (each with a word or phrase related to peace, e.g., "Trust God," "Breathe," "Pray," "Forgive")
- Envelopes (one per group)
- Timer

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that peace isn’t the absence of conflict—it’s **trusting God in the middle of chaos** (Philippians 4:6–7). Ask, *"What’s one thing that steals your peace?"* (e.g., exams, arguments, social media).
2. **Game Setup**: Split students into groups of 3–4. Give each group an envelope with **mixed-up puzzle pieces**.
3. **Challenge**: Groups have **3 minutes** to **assemble the puzzle** and discuss what each phrase means for finding peace.
4. **Share**: Groups take turns explaining **one phrase** and how it helps them find peace (e.g., "‘Breathe’ reminds me to pause when I’m stressed").
5. **Reflection**: Ask, *"What’s one ‘peace thief’ in your life? How can you combat it this week?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Provide a **pre-assembled puzzle** and have students discuss the phrases.
- **Extended**: Have groups **create their own puzzle pieces** with additional peace-related phrases.
- **Large Groups**: Split into pairs to discuss one phrase each.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British examples (e.g., "‘Forgive’—like when your mate takes the last biscuit and you’re hangry").
- Reference UK school life (e.g., "‘Pray’—like before a GCSE exam").

### **4. Patience (Galatians 5:22–23, Romans 12:12, Colossians 3:12–13, James 5:7–8)**
**Activity: "The Marshmallow Test"**
**Time**: 10–12 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Marshmallows or small sweets (one per student)
- Timer
- Paper and pens

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that patience is **waiting well** (James 5:7–8). Ask, *"What’s one thing you find hard to wait for?"* (e.g., exam results, Christmas, your turn to speak).
2. **Game Setup**: Give each student a marshmallow. Explain the rules:
   - They can eat it **now** or wait **2 minutes** to get **two marshmallows**.
   - If they wait, they must **sit quietly** and not talk.
3. **Challenge**: Start the timer. After 2 minutes, reward those who waited with an extra marshmallow.
4. **Reflection**: Ask, *"What helped you wait? What made it hard?"* Tie it to patience in real life (e.g., waiting for God’s timing, not snapping at siblings).
5. **Discussion**: *"Where in your life do you need to practice patience this week?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Shorten the wait time to **1 minute**.
- **Extended**: Have students **write down one area** where they struggle with patience and **pray for God’s help**.
- **Large Groups**: Split into smaller groups to discuss their experiences.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British humor (e.g., "Waiting for the bus in the rain—now *that’s* patience!").
- Reference UK school life (e.g., "Waiting for your GCSE results—like waiting for a text back from your crush").

### **5. Kindness (Galatians 5:22–23, Ephesians 4:32, Proverbs 19:22, Luke 6:35)**
**Activity: "The Kindness Bingo"**
**Time**: 10–12 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Printed bingo cards (with kindness actions, e.g., "Compliment someone," "Help a teacher," "Sit with someone new")
- Pens
- Small prizes (e.g., sweets, stickers)

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that kindness is **active and intentional** (Ephesians 4:32). Ask, *"What’s one kind thing someone did for you recently?"*
2. **Game Setup**: Give each student a bingo card. Explain the rules:
   - They have **5 minutes** to complete as many kindness actions as possible.
   - They must **get a signature** from the person they helped or witnessed them doing the action.
3. **Challenge**: Start the timer. Students mingle and complete their bingo cards.
4. **Share**: Ask for volunteers to share **one kind action** they did or witnessed.
5. **Reflection**: *"How did it feel to show kindness? How can you practice kindness this week?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Provide **3–4 actions** instead of a full bingo card.
- **Extended**: Have students **create their own kindness bingo cards** for the next week.
- **Large Groups**: Split into smaller groups to discuss their experiences.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British examples (e.g., "Hold the door open for someone—like a proper British gent").
- Reference UK school life (e.g., "Help a classmate with their homework—no matter how much they annoy you").

### **6. Goodness (Galatians 5:22–23, Psalm 23:6, Romans 15:14, Micah 6:8)**
**Activity: "The Goodness Debate"**
**Time**: 12–15 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Printed debate prompts (e.g., "Is it good to tell a white lie to protect someone’s feelings?" "Is it good to give money to a homeless person if they might spend it on drugs?")
- Whiteboard or large paper
- Pens

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that goodness is **doing what’s right, even when it’s hard** (Micah 6:8). Ask, *"What’s one ‘good’ thing you’ve done that was difficult?"*
2. **Game Setup**: Split students into two teams. Assign each team a **debate prompt**.
3. **Challenge**: Teams have **3 minutes** to prepare arguments for their side. Encourage them to use **Bible verses** (e.g., "Love your neighbor" for helping the homeless).
4. **Debate**: Each team presents their arguments. The other team can **ask one question** after each presentation.
5. **Reflection**: *"What’s one area where you struggle to do what’s good? How can you grow in this?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Provide **pre-written arguments** for students to choose from.
- **Extended**: Have students **research a Bible verse** to support their argument.
- **Large Groups**: Split into smaller groups to debate different prompts.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British examples (e.g., "Is it good to report a friend for cheating on an exam?").
- Reference UK pop culture (e.g., "Like when Harry Potter chose to do what’s right, even when it was hard").

### **7. Faithfulness (Galatians 5:22–23, Lamentations 3:22–23, Hebrews 11:1, Matthew 25:21)**
**Activity: "The Faithfulness Tower"**
**Time**: 10–12 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Building blocks (e.g., Jenga, LEGO, or paper cups)
- Timer

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that faithfulness is **being reliable and trustworthy** (Lamentations 3:22–23). Ask, *"Who’s one person you can always count on? Why?"*
2. **Game Setup**: Split students into groups of 3–4. Give each group **10 building blocks**.
3. **Challenge**: Groups have **3 minutes** to build the **tallest tower** they can. The catch? They must **take turns adding one block at a time**—no rushing!
4. **Share**: Groups show off their towers. Discuss what made the tower strong (e.g., patience, teamwork).
5. **Reflection**: *"How is faithfulness like building a tower? What’s one way you can be more faithful this week?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Use **larger blocks** or **pre-built structures** to modify.
- **Extended**: Have students **write a prayer** asking God to help them grow in faithfulness.
- **Large Groups**: Split into smaller groups to build multiple towers.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British humor (e.g., "Faithfulness is like a good cup of tea—it’s reliable and never lets you down!").
- Reference UK school life (e.g., "Faithfulness is showing up to youth group even when you’d rather stay home").

### **8. Gentleness (Galatians 5:22–23, 1 Peter 3:15, Proverbs 15:1, Matthew 11:29)**
**Activity: "The Gentleness Jenga"**
**Time**: 10–12 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Jenga set
- Printed "gentleness challenges" (e.g., "Say something kind to the person on your left," "Share a time you felt gentle love," "Pray for someone who’s hurt you")
- Timer

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that gentleness is **strength under control** (Proverbs 15:1). Ask, *"What’s one situation where you wish you’d been gentler?"*
2. **Game Setup**: Split students into groups of 3–4. Set up a Jenga tower for each group.
3. **Challenge**: Students take turns pulling a block. Before they pull, they must **complete a gentleness challenge** (e.g., "Say something kind to the person on your left").
4. **Share**: After the game, ask, *"Which challenge was hardest? Why?"*
5. **Reflection**: *"How can you practice gentleness this week?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Use **pre-written challenges** on the blocks.
- **Extended**: Have students **create their own gentleness challenges** to add to the game.
- **Large Groups**: Split into smaller groups to play multiple games.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British examples (e.g., "Gentleness is not snapping at your sibling when they steal your last biscuit").
- Reference UK pop culture (e.g., "Like when Paddington Bear is gentle, even when things go wrong").

### **9. Self-Control (Galatians 5:22–23, 2 Timothy 1:7, Proverbs 25:28, Titus 2:11–12)**
**Activity: "The Self-Control Obstacle Course"**
**Time**: 12–15 minutes
**Materials Needed**:
- Obstacle course props (e.g., cones, hula hoops, chairs, blindfolds)
- Timer
- Printed "self-control challenges" (e.g., "Walk backward through the course," "Only use your non-dominant hand," "Count to 5 before moving to the next obstacle")

**Instructions**:
1. **Introduction**: Explain that self-control is **mastering your desires** (Proverbs 25:28). Ask, *"What’s one area where you struggle with self-control?"* (e.g., screen time, gossip, procrastination).
2. **Game Setup**: Set up a simple obstacle course. Split students into teams.
3. **Challenge**: Each team must **complete the course**, but they must **follow a self-control challenge** at each obstacle (e.g., "Count to 5 before moving").
4. **Share**: Teams discuss which challenges were hardest and why.
5. **Reflection**: *"How is self-control like an obstacle course? What’s one area where you need God’s help with self-control this week?"*

**Adaptations**:
- **Simplified**: Shorten the course or **remove some challenges**.
- **Extended**: Have students **create their own self-control challenges** for the course.
- **Large Groups**: Split into smaller groups to run multiple courses.

**UK Tie-In**:
- Use British humor (e.g., "Self-control is not eating the entire packet of Hobnobs in one sitting").
- Reference UK school life (e.g., "Self-control is not checking your phone during a boring assembly").
