# Youth Bible Course – “The Fruits of the Holy Spirit”  
**Target group:** Secondary-school students (Years 7-11) in the United Kingdom  
**Length:** 9 weekly lessons – 60-90 minutes each  

> **Core Scripture:** Galatians 5:22-23 – “But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control.”  

The curriculum follows a logical flow (Love → Joy → Peace → Patience → Kindness & Goodness → Faithfulness → Gentleness → Self-Control → Full-Fruit Review). Each lesson builds on the previous one, helping students see how the fruits work together in everyday life.

---

## Lesson-by-Lesson Overview

| Lesson | Fruit(s) | Title (Teen-Friendly) | Main Objective |
|--------|----------|-----------------------|----------------|
| 1 | **Love** | **“Love-It-Like-Jesus”** | Understand that Christ-style love is an active, self-sacrificial choice that identifies us as His disciples. |
| 2 | **Joy** | **“Joy-Fuel”** | Discover how lasting joy comes from trusting God, not from circumstances or social media. |
| 3 | **Peace** | **“Peace-Points”** | Learn how the peace Jesus gives steadies the heart in a busy, pressure-filled school life. |
| 4 | **Patience** | **“Patience-Practice”** | See patience as a spiritual workout that builds character while we wait for God’s timing. |
| 5 | **Kindness & Goodness** | **“Kind-Goodness in Action”** | Pair kindness (Eph 4:32) with goodness (Ps 23:6) to show how caring deeds flow into moral excellence. |
| 6 | **Faithfulness** | **“Faithful-Forward”** | Explore what it means to be a reliable steward of God’s gifts in school, sport, and friendships. |
| 7 | **Gentleness** | **“Gentle-Strength”** | Reveal gentleness as true strength that respects others and reflects Jesus’ humility. |
| 8 | **Self-Control** | **“Self-Control-Check”** | Equip students with practical tools to master impulses in a digital-age world. |
| 9 | **All Nine Fruits** | **“Full-Fruit Review & Commitment”** | Tie the whole series together, celebrate growth, and set personal Spirit-led goals. |

### ### Lesson 1 – Love  
**Objective:** Students will recognise that love is the root of every other fruit and will practise a concrete act of Christ-like love in school.  

#### Duration (≈ 75 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & roll-call | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Love-Note Sprint” | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (John 13:34-35) | 20 min |
| Illustration – TikTok “#LoveInAction” video | 5 min |
| Devotion & personal reflection | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (5 Q) | 15 min |
| Commitment cards & wrap-up | 10 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Love-Note Sprint  
Each student receives a stack of colourful sticky notes. In 2 minutes they write a short encouraging note (no name) and slip it into a “Love-Box” placed at the front of the room. After the activity, the facilitator reads a few aloud, showing how a tiny act can brighten a day.

#### Bible Teaching  
> **John 13:34-35 (NIV)**  
> 34 “A new command I give you: **Love one another**. As I have loved you, **you also must love one another**.  
> 35 **By this everyone will know** that you are my disciples, **if you love one another**.”  

*Historical-cultural note*: spoken at the Last Supper, a Passover feast where loyalty to one’s own clan was the norm. Jesus flips that expectation, giving a **new command** that marks true discipleship.  

*Key words*: **new commandment** (*kainē entolē*), **love** (*agapē* – self-sacrificial), **by this** – love is the visible proof of being a follower.  

*Application*: In a UK school, love looks like inviting a new pupil to sit with you at lunch, defending a classmate from online bullying, or simply listening without judgment.  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **TikTok/YouTube short (30 sec)** – montage of students slipping love-notes, sharing a snack, helping with homework, captioned “Love in Action – John 13:34-35”.  
* **Graphic poster** – “Love = Action + Choice” with icons (handshake, heart, open door).  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“When Jesus said ‘Love one another as I have loved you’, He wasn’t talking about a warm feeling that fades when the Wi-Fi drops. He was giving us a **new command** that makes the world see we belong to Him. Think of the first day you walked into a new school. The nervousness, the cliques, the “who’s-who” map. Imagine someone handing you a note that said, ‘You’re welcome here.’ That tiny act changes the whole day. Today, let’s be the ones who hand out those notes. Write a quick encouraging text to a friend who’s been quiet in class, or share your revision notes with someone who missed a lesson. When we choose love over indifference, we become the living proof that we are Jesus’ disciples.*  

#### Group Discussion Questions (5)  
1. What does “love one another as I have loved you” look like in a school hallway?  
2. How can you tell the difference between “liking” someone and the **self-sacrificial love** Jesus describes?  
3. Share a time you felt loved by a peer or teacher – how can that shape the love you give?  
4. What obstacles (social media, peer pressure) make it hard to love actively, and how can we overcome them?  
5. How will you show love this week? (Write a concrete plan on your card.)  

#### Guidance for Youth Team Helpers  
* **Time-keeping:** Use a visible timer for the Love-Note Sprint; give a 1-minute warning before each segment ends.  
* **Facilitation tip:** Model the “love note” yourself first, then invite volunteers to read theirs aloud – this lowers anxiety.  
* **Sensitive topics:** If a student shares a story of bullying, acknowledge it, pray briefly, and offer follow-up with a trusted teacher or pastoral lead.  
* **Encouragement:** Praise even small attempts; remind them that love is a habit, not a feeling.  

#### Materials & Resources  
* Sticky-note pads, pens, “Love-Box” (basket).  
* Projector/screen for TikTok video.  
* Printed handout: **John 13:34-35 – Key Words & Quick-Apply Sheet**.  
* Commitment cards (A5) for weekly love-action plan.

### ### Lesson 2 – Joy  
**Objective:** Students will recognise that true joy is a constant, God-rooted confidence, not a fleeting mood, and will learn a simple “Joy-Reset” habit for stressful moments.  

#### Duration (≈ 75 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & check-in | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Joy-Snap” (share a photo that makes you happy) | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (Phil 4:4) | 20 min |
| Illustration – “Joy-Fuel” graphic (battery icon) | 5 min |
| Devotion (150-200 words) | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (4 Q) | 15 min |
| Joy-Reset challenge & wrap-up | 10 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Joy-Snap  
Students pull a random “Joy-Snap” card with a prompt (e.g., “Show a picture of your favourite place in the UK”). They share why it brings them joy; the group notes common themes (nature, music, friends).  

#### Bible Teaching  
> **Philippians 4:4 (NIV)**  
> “**Rejoice in the Lord always**. Again I say, **rejoice!**”

*Historical-cultural note*: Paul writes from a Roman prison to a church in Philippi, a city of merchants and festivals. “Joy” (*chara*) in that context meant a deep, enduring gladness rooted in Christ, not the fleeting happiness of a party.  

*Key words*: **rejoice** (*agalláō*) – an active, intentional celebration; **always** (*panta*) – a continual posture.  

*Application*: In a UK school, joy can be cultivated by **starting each lesson with a quick thank-you prayer**, listening to a worship song before a test, or keeping a “Joy-Journal” of three good things each day.  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **Graphic:** Battery icon labelled “Joy-Fuel – Powered by Praise, Prayer, Perspective”.  
* **Short video (1 min)** – a montage of students dancing after a sports win, then pausing to thank God.  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“Paul’s command to ‘rejoice always’ sounds impossible when you’re juggling GCSEs, homework, and the pressure to look cool on Instagram. Yet the joy he talks about isn’t dependent on circumstances; it’s a **choice to celebrate God’s presence**. Imagine your spirit as a phone battery. When you plug into God through a quick prayer or a worship song, the battery charges, and you can face the next lesson with a smile. Try the ‘Joy-Reset’: when you feel stressed, pause, take three deep breaths, say ‘Thank you, God, for this moment’, and smile at yourself in the mirror. You’ll notice a shift – the anxiety loosens, and you’re ready to tackle the next task.”  

#### Group Discussion Questions (4)  
1. What does “rejoice always” look like when you’re stressed about exams?  
2. How can you create a “joy-fuel” habit that fits into your daily school routine?  
3. Share a moment this week when you felt God’s joy despite a bad day.  
4. Which “battery-draining” habits (social media, comparison) need to be turned off?  

#### Guidance for Youth Team Helpers  
* **Encourage participation:** Allow shy students to write their joy-journal entry on a piece of paper rather than speak aloud.  
* **Manage time:** Keep the Joy-Snap to 30 seconds per student to stay on schedule.  
* **Sensitive handling:** If a student shares a heavy burden (e.g., family trouble), acknowledge, pray, and arrange pastoral follow-up.  

#### Materials & Resources  
* “Joy-Snap” cards (printed prompts).  
* Battery-fuel graphic handout.  
* Small notebook for each student (Joy-Journal).  
* Projector for video.

### ### Lesson 3 – Peace  
**Objective:** Students will discover how the peace Jesus offers steadies the heart amid academic pressure, friendships, and online conflict.  

#### Duration (≈ 80 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & quick prayer | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Calm-Countdown” (count down from 30 seconds while breathing) | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (John 14:27) | 20 min |
| Illustration – “Peace-Pause” animation (YouTube) | 5 min |
| Devotion (150-200 words) | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (5 Q) | 15 min |
| Peace-Pause practice & wrap-up | 15 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Calm-Countdown  
Students sit in a circle, close their eyes, and count down silently from 30 to 1, breathing slowly. After the exercise, ask how their bodies feel.  

#### Bible Teaching  
> **John 14:27 (NIV)**  
> “**Peace I leave with you; my peace I give to you.** I do not give it to you as the world does. **Do not let your hearts be distressed or lacking in courage.**”

*Historical-cultural note*: Jesus speaks to his disciples in the Upper Room, shortly before his crucifixion, offering a peace that transcends the political turmoil of Roman-occupied Judea.  

*Key words*: **peace** (*eirēnē*) – a deep, inner calm; **world’s peace** – temporary, based on circumstances; **distressed** (*tarassomai*) – anxiety; **courage** (*tharseō*) – boldness.  

*Application*: In a UK school, this peace can be accessed by **taking a “Peace-Pause” before a stressful test**, using a short breathing exercise, or remembering that God’s calm is “not like the world’s” – it stays even when the Wi-Fi drops or a friend argues online.  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **“Peace-Pause” animation (1 min): a student in a noisy hallway presses a “pause” button, the noise fades, and a calm voice says the verse.  
* **Poster:** “World’s peace = temporary; Jesus’ peace = permanent – hold onto it.”  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“Imagine you’re about to sit an important GCSE maths paper. Your heart races, your palms sweat – the world’s ‘peace’ would be a quick scroll through memes to calm down. Jesus, however, offers a **different kind of peace** that stays even when the exam timer ticks. He says, ‘Do not let your hearts be distressed.’ That means you can **choose** to pause, breathe, and remind yourself: ‘God is with me.’ Try the ‘Peace-Pause’ now: inhale for four counts, hold four, exhale four. Do it three times, then open your eyes. Notice the shift? That’s the Spirit’s peace guarding your mind, ready to be used whenever anxiety creeps in – in the classroom, on the bus, or in a heated group chat.”  

#### Group Discussion Questions (5)  
1. When have you felt “distressed” at school, and how did you respond?  
2. What does “peace that the world does not give” look like in your daily life?  
3. How can you remind yourself of Jesus’ peace before a stressful event?  
4. Share a practical “Peace-Pause” technique you could use during a heated online argument.  
5. How can you be a “peace-bringer” for a friend who is anxious?  

#### Guidance for Youth Team Helpers  
* **Model the Peace-Pause** before starting the lesson.  
* **Time management:** Use a timer for the Calm-Countdown and the animation.  
* **Sensitive handling:** If a student reveals severe anxiety or mental-health concerns, listen, affirm, and refer to school counselling.  
* **Encourage sharing:** Allow those comfortable to demonstrate the breathing technique for the group.  

#### Materials & Resources  
* Projector & speakers for animation.  
* Handout: “Peace-Pause Steps”.  
* Small cards with the verse for each student.

### ### Lesson 4 – Patience  
**Objective:** Students will view patience as a spiritual discipline that strengthens character while they wait for God’s timing (exam results, friendships, personal goals).  

#### Duration (≈ 75 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & quick check-in | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Patience-Pixel” (assemble a puzzle in teams) | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (James 5:7-8) | 20 min |
| Illustration – “Farmer-Harvest” storyboard (6 panels) | 5 min |
| Devotion (150-200 words) | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (5 Q) | 15 min |
| Patience-Practice pledge & wrap-up | 10 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Patience-Pixel  
Teams receive a small jigsaw (30-piece) and must finish it **without looking at the picture**. The activity highlights the need for steady, patient effort.  

#### Bible Teaching  
> **James 5:7-8 (NIV)**  
> 7 “**Be patient**, therefore, brothers and sisters, **until the Lord’s coming**. See how the farmer waits for the precious fruit of the earth, **being patient** about it, until it receives the early and late rains.  
> 8 You also **be patient**, **strengthen your hearts**, for the **Lord’s coming is near**.”

*Historical-cultural note*: James addresses early-Christian communities facing persecution and economic hardship. The farmer’s image resonated with a largely agrarian society that knew the long wait for harvest.  

*Key words*: **patient** (*makrothumeō*) – purposeful waiting; **precious fruit** – the reward of God’s kingdom; **strengthen your hearts** – build inner resilience.  

*Application*: In a UK school, patience shows up when **waiting for exam results**, **studying a subject that feels hard**, or **giving a friend space to grow**. The farmer’s example teaches that **good things take time** and that we can **strengthen our hearts** through prayer and community.  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **Storyboard (6 panels)** – a teen planting a seed (goal), watering (study), facing rain (setbacks), finally harvesting (achievement).  
* **Graphic:** “Patience = Trust + Action + Heart-Strength”.  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“Think of the last time you waited for a text reply that never came. The waiting felt endless, right? James reminds us that **God’s people are called to be patient**, just like a farmer who knows the seed needs rain before it can become fruit. Patience isn’t passive; it’s **active trust**. While you wait for your GCSE results, you can **strengthen your heart** by praying, reviewing past papers, and encouraging a friend who’s also waiting. The longer you stay faithful in the waiting, the more you’ll see God’s character developing in you – calm, hopeful, and resilient.”*  

#### Group Discussion Questions (5)  
1. What is something you are currently “waiting” for (exam results, friendship, skill)?  
2. How does the farmer’s patience inspire you to handle your own waiting periods?  
3. What practical steps can you take to “strengthen your heart” while you wait?  
4. Share a time you gave up too quickly; what would you do differently now?  
5. How can you encourage a peer who is struggling with patience?  

#### Guidance for Youth Team Helpers  
* **Facilitate the puzzle activity** – remind teams that rushing leads to mistakes; encourage calm communication.  
* **Time-keeping:** Keep each discussion segment to 3 minutes per question.  
* **Sensitive topics:** If a student shares a serious anxiety about results, validate feelings and offer pastoral follow-up.  
* **Encourage commitment:** Have each student write a “Patience-Practice” pledge (e.g., “I will pray each night while waiting for my exam results”).  

#### Materials & Resources  
* Small jigsaw puzzles (one per team).  
* Storyboard printed on A3 paper.  
* Patience-Practice pledge cards.

### ### Lesson 5 – Kindness & Goodness  
**Objective:** Students will see how everyday kindness flows into a life of moral goodness, reflecting God’s character in school and online.  

#### Duration (≈ 85 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & quick prayer | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Kindness-Chain” (each student adds a link) | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (Eph 4:32 & Ps 23:6) | 25 min |
| Illustration – “Good-Deed Relay” video (YouTube) | 5 min |
| Devotion (150-200 words) | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (5 Q) | 15 min |
| Kind-Goodness challenge & wrap-up | 15 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Kindness-Chain  
A roll of paper is passed around; each student writes one kind act they have done this week, then folds it and adds it to the chain. At the end, the chain is displayed as a visual reminder of collective kindness.  

#### Bible Teaching  
> **Ephesians 4:32 (NIV)**  
> “**Be kind** to one another, **compassionate**, forgiving one another, just as **God in Christ also forgave you**.”  

> **Psalm 23:6 (NIV)**  
> “Surely **goodness** and **faithfulness** will pursue me all my days, and I will dwell in the house of the Lord forever.”  

*Historical-cultural note*: Paul writes to a church in a bustling, competitive city (Ephesus). Kindness (*chrestos*) was counter-cultural. The Psalm, attributed to David, uses the shepherd metaphor familiar to an agrarian Israelite audience; “goodness” (*tov*) and “faithfulness” (*emunah*) are active, pursuing forces.  

*Key words*: **kind** (*chrestos*), **compassionate** (*splanchnizomai*), **goodness** (*tov*), **faithfulness** (*emunah*).  

*Application*: In a UK school, **kindness** is sharing notes, offering a seat, or sending an encouraging DM. **Goodness** is doing the right thing even when no one watches – e.g., refusing to cheat, standing up for a bullied peer, or being honest in group work.  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **“Good-Deed Relay” video (2 min)** – students pass a ball while performing quick kind acts (handing a snack, writing a note).  
* **Graphic:** “Kindness → Goodness → God’s Pursuit” with arrows and icons (heart, shield).  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“When David says ‘goodness and faithfulness will pursue me’, he’s describing a life where **good deeds chase you** like a loyal dog. In school, that could be the teacher who notices you always helping others, or a friend who trusts you with a secret because you’re known for honesty. Kindness is the first step – a smile, a shared textbook – and it **creates a momentum** that leads to deeper goodness: standing up for what’s right, refusing to gossip, and being reliable. Let today be the day you **start a kindness chain** that God’s goodness will chase after you all the way to the end of the school year.”  

#### Group Discussion Questions (5)  
1. What simple act of kindness could you do for a classmate today?  
2. How does “goodness” differ from “kindness,” and why does it matter in exams or group projects?  
3. Share a time you saw someone’s goodness “pursue” them (e.g., they were rewarded for honesty).  
4. How can you guard against the temptation to cut corners (cheat) when the pressure is high?  
5. What personal “Kind-Goodness” challenge will you set for the next week?  

#### Guidance for Youth Team Helpers  
* **Facilitate the Kindness-Chain** – ensure each student writes something genuine; avoid “I didn’t do anything” by prompting with examples.  
* **Video timing:** Keep the Good-Deed Relay under 2 minutes; use it as a visual energiser.  
* **Sensitive issues:** If a student admits to having cheated before, respond with grace, encourage confession to God, and discuss steps toward integrity.  
* **Encourage accountability:** Pair students to check in on each other’s Kind-Goodness challenges.  

#### Materials & Resources  
* Roll of paper & pens for Kindness-Chain.  
* Printed handout with both verses and key-word definitions.  
* “Good-Deed Relay” video (pre-loaded on a USB).  
* Challenge cards (one per student) – write a specific kindness & goodness goal.

### ### Lesson 6 – Faithfulness  
**Objective:** Students will understand that being a faithful steward means keeping promises, showing reliability, and honoring God’s gifts in everyday life.  

#### Duration (≈ 80 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & prayer | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Faith-Flip” (share a promise kept) | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (1 Cor 4:2) | 20 min |
| Illustration – “Steward-Spotlight” (real-life examples) | 5 min |
| Devotion (150-200 words) | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (5 Q) | 15 min |
| Faithfulness pledge & wrap-up | 15 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Faith-Flip  
Students stand in a circle; each says a promise they kept this week (e.g., “I turned up for football practice”). The next person “flips” by adding a promise they intend to keep. This highlights the habit of reliability.  

#### Bible Teaching  
> **1 Corinthians 4:2 (NIV)**  
> “Now **what is required** of those **who have been entrusted** with **stewardship** is that **they be found faithful**.”

*Historical-cultural note*: Paul writes to a church dealing with divisions and questions of leadership. The Greek term **steward** (*οἰκονόμος*) referred to a household manager responsible for the master’s property.  

*Key words*: **required** (*apodeiknumi*), **entrusted** (*paratithēmi*), **faithful** (*pistos*) – trustworthy, reliable.  

*Application*: In a UK school, **faithfulness** shows up when you **turn up for a club you signed up for**, **hand in assignments on time**, or **keep a secret**. It also means **using your talents** (music, sport, art) for God’s glory, not just for personal gain.  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **Steward-Spotlight** slides – short bios of famous UK figures (e.g., Sir David Attenborough, Malala Yousafzai) who exemplify faithfulness.  
* **Graphic:** “Faithful = Promise + Action + Trust”.  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“Imagine you’re the captain of a school rowing crew. Your teammates trust you to **steer**, **set the rhythm**, and **keep the boat balanced**. If you miss practice, the whole crew suffers. Paul’s words to the Corinthians remind us that **faithfulness is the mark of a good steward** – someone who handles what’s been given to them with care. In everyday life, that could be as simple as **showing up for a study group** you promised to lead, or **keeping a friend’s confidence** even when gossip is tempting. When we are faithful, God’s trust in us grows, and He rewards us with deeper opportunities to serve.**  

#### Group Discussion Questions (5)  
1. What is a promise you find hard to keep, and why?  
2. How does being faithful to small tasks (homework, chores) prepare you for bigger responsibilities?  
3. Share an example of a public figure whose faithfulness inspires you.  
4. How can you help a friend become more faithful in their commitments?  
5. What personal “faithfulness” goal will you set for the next month?  

#### Guidance for Youth Team Helpers  
* **Encourage honesty:** When a student admits to breaking a promise, model grace and discuss practical steps to repair trust.  
* **Time-management:** Keep the Faith-Flip to 30 seconds per student.  
* **Follow-up:** Record each student’s faithfulness goal on a board; revisit in the next session.  

#### Materials & Resources  
* Slides for Steward-Spotlight (PowerPoint or Google Slides).  
* Faith-Flip prompt cards.  
* Faithfulness pledge sheets (include space for goal and date).

### ### Lesson 7 – Gentleness  
**Objective:** Students will recognise gentleness as strength-in-humility, learning to respond calmly in conflict and to offer “rest” to others.  

#### Duration (≈ 75 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & prayer | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Gentle-Gesture” (show a gentle hand movement) | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (Matt 11:29) | 20 min |
| Illustration – “Yoke-Shift” short drama (2 min) | 5 min |
| Devotion (150-200 words) | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (5 Q) | 15 min |
| Gentle-Commitment card & wrap-up | 10 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Gentle-Gesture  
Students stand in a circle; each demonstrates a **gentle hand movement** (e.g., a slow wave, a soft high-five). The group mirrors it, highlighting how gentleness can be expressed physically.  

#### Bible Teaching  
> **Matthew 11:29 (NIV)**  
> “Take my **yoke** upon you and **learn from me**, **for I am gentle and humble in heart**, and you will find **rest for your souls**.”

*Historical-cultural note*: Jesus addresses a crowd weary from the heavy legalism of the Pharisees. A **yoke** was a wooden beam joining two oxen; it symbolised shared labor. Jesus offers a **different kind of yoke**—one of **gentleness** (*praus*) and humility (*tapeinos*).  

*Key words*: **yoke** – partnership with Christ; **gentle** – meek, not weak; **humble in heart** – low-self-esteem in service; **rest** – inner peace.  

*Application*: In a UK school, **gentleness** appears when you **listen without interrupting**, **respond calmly to a teammate’s mistake**, or **use kind language in online chats**. It’s not about being a pushover; it’s about **strength under control** (e.g., a captain who corrects a player kindly).  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **“Yoke-Shift” drama** – two students act out a heavy “legalistic” yoke (rigid, stiff) then switch to a soft, flexible yoke representing Jesus’ gentleness.  
* **Poster:** “Gentle ≠ Weak – It’s Strength in Humility”.  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“Picture a busy school hallway at lunch. Voices clash, lockers slam, and tempers flare. Jesus invites us to **take his yoke**, which isn’t a heavy burden but a **soft, guiding strap** that teaches us to move together with **gentle strength**. When a friend snaps at you, you could fire back, but a gentle response—‘I hear you, let’s sort this out’—creates space for **rest** in both hearts. That rest isn’t just calm; it’s the **peace of knowing you’re walking with Christ**. Try this week: when a disagreement arises, **pause**, breathe, and answer with a gentle tone. You’ll discover that the tension eases, and the other person often mirrors your calm.*  

#### Group Discussion Questions (5)  
1. What does “gentle and humble in heart” look like when a teammate makes a mistake?  
2. How can you “take Jesus’ yoke” in a school project where opinions clash?  
3. Share a moment when someone’s gentle response changed the atmosphere for you.  
4. Why do you think people sometimes mistake gentleness for weakness?  
5. What specific “gentle” action will you practice this week?  

#### Guidance for Youth Team Helpers  
* **Model gentleness** in your own tone and body language throughout the session.  
* **Drama cue:** Keep the “Yoke-Shift” under 2 minutes; ask volunteers to rehearse quickly.  
* **Handling conflict:** If a discussion becomes heated, intervene gently, reminding the group of the verse.  

#### Materials & Resources  
* Script for “Yoke-Shift” drama (printed).  
* Gentle-Commitment cards (space for personal goal).  
* Poster printed on A3.

### ### Lesson 8 – Self-Control  
**Objective:** Students will learn practical strategies to master impulses—online, emotional, and physical—empowered by the Spirit’s power and love.  

#### Duration (≈ 80 min)  
| Activity | Time |
|----------|------|
| Welcome & prayer | 5 min |
| Ice-breaker – “Self-Control Sprint” (quick reaction game) | 10 min |
| Bible Teaching (2 Tim 1:7) | 20 min |
| Illustration – “Power-Button” video (2 min) | 5 min |
| Devotion (150-200 words) | 10 min |
| Small-group discussion (5 Q) | 15 min |
| Self-Control action plan & wrap-up | 15 min |

#### Ice-breaker – Self-Control Sprint  
Students stand in two lines. The leader shouts a command (“jump”, “freeze”, “clap”). The first to react correctly gets a point. The game shows how quickly we can act on impulse and how we can choose to pause.  

#### Bible Teaching  
> **2 Timothy 1:7 (NIV)**  
> “For **God did not give us a spirit of fear**, but of **power**, **love**, and **self-control**.”

*Historical-cultural note*: Paul writes to Timothy, a young pastor in Ephesus, encouraging him to stand firm against false teaching and persecution. The Roman world prized **courage** (often expressed as fearlessness), but Paul redirects the focus to **self-mastery**.  

*Key words*: **fear** (*phobos*) – paralysing anxiety; **power** (*dunamis*) – divine ability; **love** (*agape*) – self-sacrificial; **self-control** (*egkrateia*) – mastery over desires.  

*Application*: In a UK context, **self-control** means **limiting screen time before homework**, **thinking before posting a heated comment**, **saying no to peer pressure for alcohol**, and **choosing healthy food**. The verse reminds us that **God equips us with power and love** to make those choices.  

#### Illustrations & Visual Aids  
* **“Power-Button” video** – a teen presses a giant red “self-control” button before a gaming session, the screen switches to a study timer.  
* **Graphic:** “Power + Love = Self-Control” with icons (muscle, heart, shield).  

#### Devotion & Application (≈ 180 words)  
> *“Think of the ‘pause’ button on a video player. When you’re tempted to binge-watch TikTok while a maths worksheet sits untouched, you can **press pause**—that’s self-control. Paul tells us God has given us **power** (the ability to say ‘no’) and **love** (the desire to protect ourselves and others) so we can **master our impulses**. Try the ‘30-Minute Power-Block’: set a timer for 30 minutes, turn off notifications, and focus on one task. When the timer ends, you’ve exercised self-control, and the Spirit’s power and love have helped you finish. Over time, those small blocks build a habit that protects you from the pull of instant gratification.”
