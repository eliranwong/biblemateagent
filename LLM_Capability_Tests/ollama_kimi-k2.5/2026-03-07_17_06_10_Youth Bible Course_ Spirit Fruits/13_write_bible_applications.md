**WEEK 7: PISTIS (πίστις) — COVENANT RELIABILITY**

*Scriptures: Ruth 1:16-18; Genesis 39:1-23; John 17:4; Philippians 2:8; 1 Corinthians 4:2; 2 Timothy 2:13; 2 Timothy 3:10-11*

---

**CONCRETE THINKERS (YEARS 7-8)**

**Daily Life and Personal Circumstances**
When you promise to meet your friend at the library at 4pm but Netflix releases a new series at 3:30pm, Ruth 1:16-18 confronts your choice: "Where you go I will go." Ruth had every cultural excuse to abandon Naomi—she was a Moabite widow with no obligation to an Israelite mother-in-law. Yet she clung to her. This is *pistis*—covenant reliability that keeps showing up even when circumstances change. When you are tempted to ghost a friend who has become annoying or socially embarrassing, remember Joseph in Potiphar's house (Genesis 39:1-23): he remained faithful to his integrity even when no one was watching and when faithfulness cost him his freedom. When your parents divorce and you feel tempted to withdraw from both households to punish them, 2 Timothy 2:13 anchors you: "If we are faithless, he remains faithful." Your reliability in family relationships does not depend on their perfection but on God's faithfulness to you.

**Relationships with God and Others**
Your relationship with God is characterized by His covenant faithfulness (2 Timothy 2:13)—He remains faithful even when you are not. This security enables you to be faithful to others without calculating reciprocity. When your sports team loses every match and you want to quit, Philippians 2:8 ("obedient to death") models faithfulness unto failure, not just success. In friendships, this means not benching someone because a better offer came along (the "disposable culture" of modern relationships), but being the friend who shows up for the boring hospital visit, the difficult conversation, the inconvenient favor. When you commit to group work, 1 Corinthians 4:2 ("it is required that those who have been given a trust must prove faithful") means completing your section even when others slack off, because your integrity is not determined by their laziness.

**Practical Application**
Develop a "Covenant Check" before making commitments: Ask "Am I willing to Ruth 1:16 this?" before promising to attend an event. When tempted to ghost someone, send the difficult text explaining your absence rather than disappearing, practicing Joseph's visible integrity. When family instability hits (divorce, blended family tensions), create a "Faithfulness Ritual"—a weekly phone call to the parent you see less, or a consistent presence at younger half-siblings' events, embodying 2 Timothy 2:13's assurance that God remains faithful despite your family chaos.

**Concrete Behavioral Changes**
- **Promise Protocol:** Before saying "I promise" or "I'll be there," pause and consider Ruth 1:18 ("When she saw that she was determined to go with her, she stopped urging her"). Only commit to what you can actually fulfill.
- **Anti-Ghosting:** When you want to withdraw from a friendship, practice 1 Corinthians 4:2 by having the difficult conversation rather than disappearing, even if it costs you social comfort.
- **Group Work Integrity:** Complete your assigned section 24 hours before the deadline to allow time for integration, embodying the "found faithful" standard regardless of others' performance.
- **Family Presence:** When parents divorce, resist the urge to "choose sides"; instead, schedule consistent time with both households, practicing covenant reliability that transcends their conflict.

**WEEK 8: PRAUTES (πραΰτης) — STRENGTH UNDER CONTROL**

*Scriptures: Numbers 12:3; Matthew 11:29; Matthew 19:13-15; 1 Samuel 24:1-7; 1 Samuel 26:1-12; Galatians 6:1; 2 Timothy 2:25; 1 Peter 3:15-16*

---

**CONCRETE THINKERS (YEARS 7-8)**

**Daily Life and Personal Circumstances**
When someone punches you in the playground and every muscle screams to punch back, 1 Samuel 24:1-7 shows David in the cave with Saul—he had the power to kill his enemy but cut only the corner of his robe instead. This is *prautes*—strength under control, not weakness. When you are angry at a teacher for unfair marking and want to shout in the classroom, Numbers 12:3 describes Moses as "very humble, more than anyone else on the face of the earth"—yet he led a nation. Your gentleness is not lack of strength but strength directed rightly. When younger students annoy you by hanging around your group, Matthew 19:13-15 shows Jesus welcoming children when disciples tried to shoo them away—gentleness makes space for the vulnerable without dominating them.

**Relationships with God and Others**
Matthew 11:29 invites you to take Jesus' yoke because He is "gentle and humble in heart"—this is the same Jesus who would cleanse the temple. Your relationship with God is characterized by this controlled strength: you can be honest about your anger without sinning in your anger. When a friend falls into difficulty (Galatians 6:1), *prautes* means restoring them gently, not with harsh condemnation. When someone asks about your faith in a mocking way, 1 Peter 3:15-16 requires "gentleness and respect," not aggressive argument. In multicultural conflicts—perhaps a disagreement about prayer practices or dietary rules—2 Timothy 2:25 models correcting opponents "with gentleness," recognizing that God grants repentance, not your forcefulness.

**Practical Application**
Practice the "David Pause": When tempted to retaliate (verbally or physically), physically step back and count to ten, remembering David in the cave with Saul's robe in his hand. When correcting a friend, use "I" statements rather than "You always" accusations, embodying Galatians 6:1's gentle restoration. When younger students want to join your group, practice Matthew 19:14 by making space for them rather than asserting your seniority.

**Concrete Behavioral Changes**
- **De-escalation Protocol:** When angry, lower your voice volume and slow your speech speed—physical control of voice embodies *prautes*.
- **Gentle Correction:** When a friend makes a mistake, speak to them privately before publicly correcting, practicing 2 Timothy 2:25's "gentleness" toward opponents.
- **Vulnerability Welcome:** When Year 7 students approach your group, physically make space for them to sit with you, embodying Jesus' welcome of children.
- **Conflict Transformation:** When multicultural tensions arise (e.g., disagreement about religious practices), ask questions before making statements, practicing 1 Peter 3:15's "gentleness and respect."

*Scriptures: Genesis 39:7-18; Daniel 1:8-16; Matthew 4:1-11; Matthew 26:36-46; 1 Corinthians 9:24-27; Titus 2:11-14; 2 Peter 1:5-6*

---

**CONCRETE THINKERS (YEARS 7-8)**

**Daily Life and Personal Circumstances**
When your phone buzzes with TikTok notifications during Maths homework and your thumb moves to open it before your brain catches up, Genesis 39:7-18 provides the model: Joseph refused Potiphar's wife "day after day" even though no one would know and even though he was a slave with no rights. This is *enkrateia*—mastery of desires, not denial of desires. When friends dare you to vape behind the sports block, Daniel 1:8-16 shows Daniel refusing the king's food—not because food is evil, but because he would not be mastered by appetite. When you are angry and want to smash something, Matthew 26:36-46 shows Jesus controlling His emotions—"not my will but yours"—even when He was overwhelmed. This is not suppression (pretending you don't feel) but direction (choosing how to act despite feeling).

**Relationships with God and Others**
Your relationship with God is characterized by Titus 2:11-14's "self-controlled, upright and godly lives"—the grace of God teaches you to say "no" to ungodliness. This is not grim willpower but Spirit-empowered choice. When gaming with friends and they want to continue past your agreed stop time, *enkrateia* means logging off despite FOMO (fear of missing out), because 1 Corinthians 9:27 requires "beating your body" to make it a slave, not letting it enslave you. When tempted to plagiarize because everyone else is, Daniel 1:8's "resolved not to defile himself" provides the model—pre-decision before the moment of temptation. When angry with parents, Matthew 26:39's "not my will but yours" models submitting immediate emotional reactions to larger commitments.

**Practical Application**
Create "Joseph Barriers"—physical obstacles between you and temptation. If TikTok is your struggle, delete the app (not just the account) during term time; make the barrier high enough that autopilot can't overcome it. Practice "Daniel Decisions"—pre-deciding your boundaries (no copying homework, no vaping) before the pressure moment so you don't have to reason when tempted. When angry, use the "Gethsemane Pause"—name your emotion ("I feel furious") but choose your action ("I will speak calmly or walk away"), embodying Matthew 26:39's submission of will.

**Concrete Behavioral Changes**
- **Digital Boundaries:** Use Screen Time limits that require a password (given to parent/friend) to override, creating Joseph's "day after day" resistance structure.
- **Pre-Decision:** Write your "Daniel 1:8" resolutions before term starts—specific boundaries about homework integrity, substance use, screen time—and post them visible.
- **Anger Management:** When angry, breathe four counts in, hold, out—physical regulation that creates space for Matthew 26:39's "not my will."
- **Gaming Discipline:** Set a timer visible on screen; when it sounds, log off immediately regardless of game state, practicing 1 Corinthians 9:27's "strict training."

**Daily Life and Personal Circumstances**
The singular "fruit" (καρπὸς) of Galatians 5:22 indicates that *enkrateia* is not merely the ninth virtue but the capstone that protects and integrates the other eight. Without self-control, love becomes manipulation, joy becomes hedonism, peace becomes escapism. Matthew 4:1-11 demonstrates *enkrateia* as the mastery that refuses shortcuts to legitimate goods—Jesus would not turn stones to bread (appetite), throw Himself from the temple (spectacle), or worship Satan for kingdoms (power) because He trusted the Father's timing and methods. When you are tempted to use essay mills to secure your university place (a good end by illicit means), Matthew 4:10 ("Worship the Lord your God and serve Him only") demands integrity in means, not just ends. When navigating sexual pressure in relationships, Genesis 39:7-18 models that *enkrateia* is not prudishness but power—Joseph's refusal was an assertion of his identity as a free man despite his slave status. When facing academic procrastination, 1 Corinthians 9:24-27's athletic metaphor requires "strict training" (*enkrateia* cognate) that treats the mind as a muscle to be disciplined, not a mood to be indulged.

**Relationships with God and Others**
Titus 2:11-14 connects *enkrateia* to eschatology: "We wait for the blessed hope—the appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior." Self-control is not stoic suppression but Spirit-empowered waiting for the coming glory. Your relationship with God is characterized by this "already but not yet" tension—present desires are real but not ultimate. When mentoring younger students, 2 Peter 1:5-6's sequence ("add to your faith goodness; and to goodness, knowledge; and to knowledge, self-control") indicates that *enkrateia* builds on theological knowledge, not willpower alone. When engaging with addiction (digital, substance, behavioral), recognize that *enkrateia* is Spirit-produced fruit, not self-generated achievement—yet it requires your participation in "training" (1 Corinthians 9:25). The integration of cognitive behavioral strategies (barriers, pre-decision, replacement behaviors) with Spirit-dependence is not secular psychology invading theology but wisdom applied to Spirit-empowered transformation.

**Practical Application**
Develop an "Eschatological Discipline" framework: Titus 2:13's "blessed hope" motivates present self-control not through fear (of failure, of punishment) but through promise (of glory). When tempted to compromise integrity for immediate gain (exam cheating, substance use, sexual pressure), practice the "Wilderness Three" (Matthew 4:1-11): name the legitimate need (hunger, significance, power) and identify the God-honoring path to meet it rather than the shortcut. When struggling with digital addiction, recognize that *enkrateia* protects the entire fruit—without it, you cannot cultivate love, joy, or peace because your attention is colonized by algorithmic demands.

**Concrete Behavioral Changes**
- **Integration Practice:** Weekly review how *enkrateia* protected other fruits—did phone boundaries enable presence with friends (love)? Did academic discipline create space for rest (peace)?
- **Eschatological Framing:** When tempted to immediate gratification, verbalize Titus 2:13—"I am waiting for the blessed hope"—reframing delay as anticipation rather than deprivation.
- **Spirit-Dependence:** When facing temptation, pray "Spirit, produce Your fruit of self-control" rather than "I must try harder," recognizing *enkrateia* as divine gift while taking human responsibility (Philippine paradox).
- **CBT Integration:** Use evidence-based strategies (habit stacking, environment design, implementation intentions) as "strict training" (1 Corinthians 9:27) that cooperates with Spirit-empowerment, not replaces it.
