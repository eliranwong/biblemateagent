Design an engaging, interactive ice-breaker activity for a 50-minute lesson on *Agape* Love, the first fruit of the Holy Spirit. The activity is for a group of UK secondary school students aged 11-16 and must be practical for a classroom or hall setting. The goal is to immediately involve all participants and creatively introduce the core theme of selfless, committed, and action-oriented love that seeks the best for others, as contrasted with feelings-based or self-serving love.

The activity should:
1.  Require minimal preparation and use simple, readily available materials (e.g., paper, pens, everyday objects).
2.  Be highly interactive, getting students to move, communicate, or create something together within the first 5-10 minutes of the session.
3.  Clearly create a link to the lesson's key idea: that *agape* love is a choice demonstrated through actions, not just a emotion.
4.  Be inclusive and appropriate for a diverse group where not all students may share the same faith background, focusing on universal concepts of kindness and commitment.
5.  Include a brief, simple explanation for the youth leader on how to facilitate the activity and how to transition from the ice-breaker into the main Bible teaching on *agape*.

Provide the full activity plan with a title, list of needed materials, step-by-step instructions for the leader, and the key discussion question to bridge the activity to the lesson content.

# **Ice-Breaker Activity Plan: The Commitment Bridge**

## **Title**
The Commitment Bridge

## **Learning Objective**
To experientially introduce the concept that *agape* love is a deliberate choice to act for the good of others, demonstrated through commitment and collaboration, rather than a passive feeling.

## **Needed Materials**
*   **For the activity:** One sheet of A4 paper and one pen per student.
*   **For the leader:** A whiteboard/flipchart and marker.

## **Step-by-Step Instructions (Total Time: 8-10 minutes)**

### **Part 1: The Individual Choice (2 mins)**
1.  Give each student one sheet of A4 paper and a pen.
2.  Instruct them: **"Without talking to anyone, you have 60 seconds. Crumple your paper into a ball. On the count of three, I want you to make a choice: throw your paper ball into the middle of the room, or hold onto it. Ready? 3, 2, 1... GO!"**
3.  Observe the result. Typically, most will throw it, creating a messy pile.

### **Part 2: The Collaborative Challenge (5 mins)**
1.  Say: **"Great. Now, here's a new challenge. I need everyone to get into groups of 4 or 5 with people near you. Your team's goal is to build the longest, continuous, unsupported 'bridge' you can between two chairs (or tables) using ONLY the paper balls from your team and the pens. You cannot use tape, string, or anything else. You have 4 minutes. Go!"**
2.  **Facilitation Tips:**
    *   **Be encouraging:** Move between groups, noting their strategies. Use positive, non-directive language like "Interesting approach!" or "How are you working together to solve the stability problem?"
    *   **Emphasise the constraint:** If they ask for tape, remind them they only have paper and pens. The challenge is to commit to working with what they have.
    *   **Watch for dynamics:** Note which groups start planning, who offers their paper ball first, who suggests using pens as connectors or counterweights.

### **Part 3: Group Observation & Transition (1-2 mins)**
1.  After time is up, have each group briefly show their "bridge." Applaud the effort.
2.  Gather everyone back to a seated circle. Point to the whiteboard. Draw two columns. Label the first column **"Activity 1: The Throw."** Label the second **"Activity 2: The Bridge."**
3.  Ask: **"Let's compare these two tasks. In the first one, what was the main goal?"** (Solicit answers: it was individual, fast, maybe fun/cathartic, required no thought for others). Write key words under column 1 (e.g., "Me," "Quick," "Feeling," "Mess").
4.  Then ask: **"In the second challenge, what did you NEED to do to even start building?"** Guide them to see they had to: **gather resources from everyone, plan together, commit to a shared goal, handle materials carefully, support each other's ideas.** Write these under column 2 (e.g., "Us," "Plan," "Choice," "Action," "Commitment," "Common Good").

## **Key Discussion Question for Transition to Bible Teaching**
**"In the second challenge, you had to choose to use your paper—your resource—not for your own instant thing, but for a shared goal. Was that about how you *felt* about your teammates, or about what you *chose* and *did*?"**

**Leader's Script for Transition:**
*"What you're describing—choosing to act for the good of the whole group, even if it meant giving up your own paper—that's a powerful picture of a very specific kind of love. It's not just a warm feeling that comes and goes. It's a choice followed by an action. It's committed. It seeks the best for others. In the Bible, this is called ***Agape*** love. It's the first and most important quality that God's Spirit grows in us. Today, we're going to explore what this ***Agape*** love looks like in real life—at school, online, and at home—and how it's completely different from the world's idea of love. Let's dive in."*

**Why This Activity Works:**
*   **Interactive & Kinesthetic:** It immediately gets students moving, making choices, and collaborating.
*   **Creates a Clear Contrast:** The visceral difference between the individualistic "throw" and the collaborative "build" perfectly mirrors the contrast between self-serving impulses and *agape* love.
*   **Universal & Inclusive:** The debrief uses the universal concepts of teamwork, choice, and shared goals, making it accessible to all students regardless of personal faith.
*   **Minimal Prep:** Uses only standard classroom stationery.
*   **Natural Link:** The transition question directly bridges the experiential learning to the theological concept, setting up the Bible teaching seamlessly.
